Embekka Devalaya

Located in Udunuwara, in the Kandy District, the beautiful Embekka Devalaya was built during the 14th century by King Wickremabahu III in honour of the Hindu God, Katragama, the son of Lord Shiva and Parvati. It is said to have beenon the invitation of his queen. The site comprises the devale, digge and the centre where hewisi (sacred drums) were played.

The devalaya is predominantly an open air structure, consisting of elaborately carved pillars and a wooden roof. The carved wooden pillars in the temple are believed to have come from a royal audience hall in the city. The artists of these carvings have been acclaimed for their work, as they a noted as the finest of their kind belonging to the Kandyan era. The intricate carvings include lions, swans, bulls, elephants, double-headed eagles, leaf patterns, wrestling men, soldiers, and horse riders, dancing women, mermaids and lotus motifs. 128 exceptional carvings adorn these pillars and each and every one is different from the other.

There are eight buildings within the Devalaya premises. It is said that King Vickremabhau had donated the door Frames and parts of his palace to build this Devalaya. The doors in the devalaya have no hinges but can be used like normal doors, portraying the architectural expertise of the era. Among the items of historical value which belong to the Devalaya are items presented by King Rajasinghe and by the Portuguese and some items said to have been donated by the Ehelepola Adigar.A large “Atuwa,” a structure that holds padi is also part of the temple structure.
